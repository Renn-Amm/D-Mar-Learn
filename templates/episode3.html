Episode 3: Describing Company Growth

Describing Data and Trends

Describing data accurately is essential for conveying a clear understanding of trends, patterns, and significant changes over time. In the context of company growth, being able to articulate these changes using precise language is crucial.

Structuring Your Description

A well-structured description of data should include three key components:

Statement: Clearly state the main point or trend you are discussing.
Example: "DVD sales increased dramatically."

Supporting Data: Provide specific numerical or factual details that back up your statement.
Example: "Between 2001 and 2004, sales rose from 2 to 3 million units."

Comment/Analysis: Add context or interpret the significance of the data.
Example: "This represents a 50% increase, indicating strong consumer demand during this period."

Using Appropriate Language

To describe data effectively, you should vary your language to maintain interest and precision. Here are some useful constructions:

Adjective + Noun: "There was a steady increase in revenue over the past decade."

Verb + Adverb: "Revenue grew steadily over the past decade."

Expanding Your Vocabulary

When describing increases:

Verbs: Increase, rise, climb, grow, surge

Adverbs: Dramatically, significantly, steadily, sharply, gradually

When describing decreases:

Verbs: Decrease, decline, fall, drop, plummet

Adverbs: Slowly, rapidly, noticeably, sharply, slightly

Choosing the Right Register

The level of formality in your language can vary depending on the context:

Formal Register: Use for reports, presentations, and formal discussions.

Verbs: Increase, decrease, expand, contract

Nouns: Growth, reduction, expansion, contraction

Example: "The company experienced a substantial growth in its market share."

Informal Register: Use in casual conversations or less formal settings.

Verbs: Go up, go down, pick up, slow down

Nouns: Rise, fall, boost, drop

Example: "Sales went up by 20% last year."

Mastering Prepositions of Time

When describing data over time, it’s important to use prepositions correctly to avoid confusion:

At: A specific point in time (e.g., at 5 PM, at the end of the year)

In: A longer, non-specific time period (e.g., in 2023, in the last decade)

On: A specific day or date (e.g., on Monday, on January 1st)

Providing Examples

Using examples can make your data more relatable and understandable.

Example: "The 50% growth in DVD sales from 2001 to 2004 suggests a significant shift in consumer preferences towards home entertainment, likely driven by advancements in technology and increasing availability of DVD players."

Providing Comparisons

Comparing data points can highlight the significance of changes.

Example: "While the company’s revenue grew by 20% in 2023, its competitor only saw a 5% increase, indicating a stronger market position for the company."

Visual Representation

Whenever possible, accompany your descriptions with visual aids such as graphs, charts, or tables. This helps to illustrate the data more clearly and makes it easier for the audience to grasp the trends.
